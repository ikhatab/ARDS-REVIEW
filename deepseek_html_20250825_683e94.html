Definition and Evolution

Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS) is a life-threatening form of respiratory failure characterized by diffuse alveolar damage and increased pulmonary vascular permeability.

Berlin Definition (2012) - Current Diagnostic Criteria

  • Timing: Within 1 week of a known clinical insult or new/worsening respiratory symptoms
  • Chest imaging: Bilateral opacities not fully explained by effusions, lobar/lung collapse, or nodules
  • Origin of edema: Respiratory failure not fully explained by cardiac failure or fluid overload
  • Oxygenation impairment (with PEEP ≥5 cm H₂O):
    • Mild: 200 mmHg < PaO₂/FiO₂ ≤ 300 mmHg
    • Moderate: 100 mmHg < PaO₂/FiO₂ ≤ 200 mmHg
    • Severe: PaO₂/FiO₂ ≤ 100 mmHg

The Berlin Definition refined the previous American-European Consensus Conference (AECC) criteria, improving reliability and validity for predicting outcomes.

Key Reference:

ARDS Definition Task Force. Acute respiratory distress syndrome: the Berlin Definition. JAMA 2012;307(23):2526-33.

Epidemiology

ARDS represents a significant burden in critical care medicine with an estimated incidence of 10-86 cases per 100,000 person-years globally.

Severity Incidence (%) Mortality (%)
Mild 30-50% 27%
Moderate 30-40% 32%
Severe 10-20% 45%

The COVID-19 pandemic dramatically increased the global incidence of ARDS, with approximately 20-40% of hospitalized COVID-19 patients developing ARDS.

Pathophysiology

ARDS is characterized by diffuse alveolar damage resulting from an uncontrolled inflammatory response to direct or indirect lung injury.

Key Pathophysiological Mechanisms:

  • Alveolar-capillary barrier disruption: Increased permeability leads to protein-rich pulmonary edema
  • Surfactant dysfunction: Loss of surface tension-reducing properties promotes alveolar collapse
  • Inflammatory mediator release: Neutrophil infiltration and cytokine release cause tissue damage
  • Coagulation abnormalities: Intra-alveolar fibrin deposition and microvascular thrombosis
  • Ventilator-induced lung injury (VILI): Mechanical ventilation can exacerbate lung injury through volutrauma, barotrauma, and atelectrauma

Clinical Pearl: The "Baby Lung" Concept

The aerated lung in ARDS is not a small, healthy lung but rather a small, normally aerated compartment within a diseased, non-aerated lung. This explains why even low tidal volumes can cause volutrauma if not adjusted for the reduced aerated volume.

Management

The management of ARDS requires a multifaceted approach focusing on treating the underlying cause, supportive care, and lung-protective strategies.

Ventilatory Management

SCCM/ESICM 2023 Guideline Recommendations:

  • Use low tidal volume ventilation (4-8 mL/kg predicted body weight) and target plateau pressures <30 cm H₂O (strong recommendation)
  • Use prone positioning for >12 hours daily in severe ARDS (strong recommendation)
  • Use a conservative fluid strategy after initial resuscitation (strong recommendation)
  • Use neuromuscular blocking agents for 48 hours in early severe ARDS (weak recommendation)
  • Consider corticosteroids in moderate-to-severe ARDS (weak recommendation)

Lung-Protective Ventilation Strategy:

Parameter Recommendation Rationale
Tidal Volume 4-8 mL/kg PBW Reduces volutrauma and mortality
Plateau Pressure <30 cm H₂O Reduces barotrauma
PEEP Individualized titration Prevents atelectrauma while avoiding overdistension
Driving Pressure <14 cm H₂O Strong predictor of survival
FiO₂ Minimize to <60% when possible Reduces oxygen toxicity

Key Reference:

Griffiths MJD, et al. Guidelines on the management of acute respiratory distress syndrome. BMJ Open Respir Res 2023;10:e001461.

Rescue Therapies for Refractory Hypoxemia

  • Neuromuscular blockade: Early, short-term use in severe ARDS improves oxygenation and reduces VILI
  • Prone positioning: Improves ventilation-perfusion matching and reduces mortality in severe ARDS
  • ECMO (Extracorporeal Membrane Oxygenation): Consider in selected patients with refractory hypoxemia despite optimal management
  • Inhaled pulmonary vasodilators: May improve oxygenation but no mortality benefit demonstrated

Prognosis and Outcomes

Despite advances in management, ARDS continues to have significant mortality and long-term morbidity.

Mortality Predictors:

  • Older age
  • Non-pulmonary organ dysfunction
  • Severity of hypoxemia (PaO₂/FiO₂ ratio)
  • Underlying etiology (worse with direct lung injury)
  • Comorbidities (especially chronic liver disease)

Long-Term Sequelae (Post-Intensive Care Syndrome):

  • Physical: Muscle weakness, functional impairment, reduced exercise tolerance
  • Pulmonary: Restrictive lung disease, reduced diffusion capacity
  • Cognitive: Impaired memory, executive function, and attention
  • Psychological: Depression, anxiety, and post-traumatic stress disorder

Follow-up care should include multidisciplinary rehabilitation addressing physical, cognitive, and psychological impairments.

Step-by-Step Guide to Publishing on GitHub

1 Create a GitHub Account

If you don't have a GitHub account, go to github.com and sign up for a free account.

2 Create a New Repository

After logging in, click on the "+" icon in the top right corner and select "New repository".

  • Name your repository (e.g., "ards-review")
  • Add a description (optional but recommended)
  • Choose "Public" (required for free GitHub Pages)
  • Check "Add a README file"
  • Click "Create repository"

3 Upload Your HTML File

In your newly created repository:

  • Click on "Add file" → "Upload files"
  • Drag and drop your HTML file or click to select it
  • Scroll down and click "Commit changes"

Important Note:

For GitHub Pages to work correctly, your main HTML file must be named index.html and be in the root directory of your repository.

4 Enable GitHub Pages

Go to your repository settings:

  • Click on the "Settings" tab in your repository
  • In the left sidebar, click on "Pages"
  • Under "Source", select "Deploy from a branch"
  • Under "Branch", select "main" and folder "/ (root)"
  • Click "Save"

5 Wait for Deployment

It may take a few minutes for your site to be published. Refresh the GitHub Pages settings page until you see a green confirmation message with your site's URL.

Your website will be available at: https://[your-username].github.io/[repository-name]/

6 Update Your Content

To update your webpage in the future:

  • Go to your repository
  • Click on your HTML file
  • Click the pencil icon to edit
  • Make your changes and commit them
  • GitHub Pages will automatically update your site (may take a few minutes)

Pro Tips

  • You can use a custom domain name if you have one
  • GitHub Pages supports custom themes and Jekyll templates
  • Your repository can contain multiple HTML files for a multi-page website
  • You can collaborate with others by adding them as contributors
